Retail

Data protection across 300+ stores.

Head of Data Protection for Whole Foods Market.

After the acquisition, the reality was hundreds of stores, each holding its own customer and personal data, siloed store by store, shaped by years of local decisions. Nobody could answer the questions that matter most in data protection: who touches this data, through which services, for how long, and where would we not notice if something went wrong. The honest starting posture was that we were protecting data we could not fully see.

The mandate was deliberately not consolidation. Forcing everything into one place would have meant years of disruption to running stores for a tidiness the business did not need. The mandate was visibility. Make the data landscape legible first, especially for personal information, and let the controls follow from what we actually found rather than from what an architecture diagram assumed.

This became one of my first major AI projects, before that was a fashionable sentence. The data estate was too large and too varied for humans to map by hand, so I used in-house AI tooling to synthesize and classify data lineage at scale, tracing where data originated and how it moved and was used. On top of that map we baselined normal access behavior with analytics and alerted on the anomalies, so detection was grounded in how each store actually operated rather than a generic rule set.

Once we could see the landscape, the fixes were almost the easy part. Strong encryption went onto the most sensitive data first, because now we knew where it was. Audit readiness stopped being a scramble and became a property of the system. And data-exfiltration risk fell sharply, not because we bought a new wall, but because for the first time we could see the whole floor. Visibility was the control. Everything else was follow-through.
