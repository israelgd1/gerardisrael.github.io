Healthcare

Security across a 300-site care and pharmacy network.

Head of Infrastructure and Clinic Security for One Medical and Amazon Pharmacy.

I led infrastructure and clinic security across more than 300 healthcare sites, where the systems behind the network support patient care and prescription fulfillment. Most of that work I did without standing in a single building. I learned the landscape the slow way: a site's IT manager on a call, going one device at a time, first confirming we were looking at the right machine, then working down into how it was actually configured.

The pattern that emerged was rarely a single dramatic flaw. It was quieter than that. Controls that varied from site to site because each location had grown up on its own. Configurations that had drifted from policy with nobody watching. Segmentation that someone started and never finished. Vendor access that stayed broad because narrowing it felt risky. And underneath all of it, the most understandable reason security debt accumulates in healthcare: nobody wants to touch a system that is in the middle of supporting patient care.

So I worked the problem backwards. I treated each recurring failure mode as a fixed point and built the guardrail that made it impossible, without taking anything offline. Controls standardized across the fleet so a site's security no longer depended on its history. Secure-by-default cloud patterns, so new infrastructure arrived safe instead of being repaired later. Network segmentation and device authentication designed in rather than bolted on. Vendor access gated behind internal approval instead of standing trust. PHI handling reviewed end to end, including the unglamorous workflows where accidental exposure actually happens. Redundant paths and monitoring, so resilience was a property of the network and not a hope.

The outcome was a fleet where risk was visible, owned, and shrinking, and where security stopped being the thing that showed up after an incident and became part of how sites were built and run. In an environment where the systems cannot stop, that is the only version of security that works.
