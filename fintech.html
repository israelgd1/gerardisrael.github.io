A security program built through a $1B acquisition.

First security hire at tastytrade, through its acquisition by IG Group under FINRA scrutiny.

I joined as the first and only dedicated security hire, brought in to build a real program ahead of a $1B acquisition and under active regulatory scrutiny. My first weeks were an inventory of decisions that had been made before I arrived. There was an EDR deployed across the fleet with nobody watching it and alerting never turned on. There was a SIEM that had been bought, racked, and never configured. On paper the company had security tooling. In practice it had receipts.

That taught me something I have carried ever since: security you purchase but never operationalize is indistinguishable from no security at all, except that it is more expensive and everyone believes they are covered. The exposure was not a missing tool. It was every earlier moment where a decision got made without anyone in the room whose job was to ask how it fails.

So I built the program the other way, starting from how a trading platform actually gets hurt. I stood the tooling up properly and put real eyes behind it. I built a product-security framework so new features arrived reviewed instead of retrofitted. And I created a two-track threat-intelligence function shaped around our two real threat surfaces: one side watched customer-account fraud and takeover, because this was a platform where people manage real money, and the other tracked application vulnerabilities in the wild, which mattered within the year when Log4j landed and we already knew our exposure while others were still finding theirs.

The program held up through the acquisition and the regulatory scrutiny that came with it. After the deal closed I ran North American security on a follow-the-sun model with the acquirer's global team, and the program we had built from nothing became their entry point into the US market. A security function that started as one hire ended up carrying part of the business case.
